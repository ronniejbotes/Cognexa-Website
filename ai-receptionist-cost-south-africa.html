What does an AI receptionist cost in South Africa? (2026 guide)

By the Cognexa team · 22 July 2026

An off-the-shelf AI receptionist typically costs about R800–R5,000 a month, or roughly R1.80–R3.30 per minute on usage-based plans — well below the R8,000+ monthly cost of employing a receptionist in South Africa. Custom builds, including Cognexa’s, are quoted after a free scoping call as one fixed monthly fee in Rand, with no minimum contract.

How much does an AI receptionist cost in South Africa?

There's no single number, because "AI receptionist" covers everything from a basic chatbot widget to a fully integrated voice system that books jobs into your calendar. But published pricing from real vendors gives a useful range. Off-the-shelf AI receptionist platforms typically run from around R800 to R5,000 a month depending on call volume, or roughly R1.80 to R3.30 a minute on usage-based plans. Those figures come from Rosie's published US dollar plans ($49–$299 a month) and Bland AI's per-minute rates ($0.11–$0.14), converted at roughly R16.50 to the US dollar — an approximate rate, since none of these platforms bill in Rand directly and your actual cost will move with the exchange rate.

Custom-built AI receptionists — trained on your services, your booking system, your way of qualifying a caller — don't fit neatly into a published price list, because the cost depends on integration work, not just minutes answered. That's true whether you're comparing a voice agent, an IVR menu or a human receptionist.

What makes one AI receptionist quote higher than another?

Before comparing prices, it helps to know what actually moves them. The same platform can cost R800 a month for one business and R8,000 for another, because of:

  • Channels. Voice-only is cheaper than voice plus WhatsApp and web chat answering the same enquiries.
  • Volume. Every platform we checked prices in bands — minutes, calls or conversations — and the per-unit cost usually drops as volume rises.
  • Integrations. Reading and writing to a calendar, CRM, dispatch board or POS system is the difference between "answers the phone" and "actually runs your front desk" — see workflow automation for what that connective work looks like.
  • Script complexity. A receptionist that answers three FAQs is simpler to build than one that qualifies a lead, checks availability, quotes a price range and books a slot.
  • Language and voice. English-only is standard on most platforms; Afrikaans, isiZulu or Xhosa support varies vendor to vendor and can add cost.
  • Number and telephony setup. A new dedicated number is usually simpler (and cheaper) than porting or forwarding an existing landline.

Is an AI receptionist cheaper than hiring a receptionist?

On salary alone, yes, usually by a wide margin — but the comparison needs the full picture. Indeed's South African salary data puts the average receptionist wage at R8,017 a month, based on over 750 reported salaries. PayScale's figure is close: R107,300 a year, or roughly R8,942 a month on average, with a range of R56,000 to R191,000 a year (about R4,700 to R15,900 a month) depending on experience and location.

That's gross salary only. On top, an employer typically adds UIF contributions, paid leave, sick leave, public holiday cover, and the recruitment and training cost every time someone leaves. A receptionist also works set hours — evenings, weekends and lunch breaks go to voicemail unless you pay for a second person or overtime.

By contrast, even Rosie's top consumer plan (R4,934 a month for 2,000 minutes) or Ruby's entry-level human virtual receptionist plan (R4,125 a month for 50 minutes) sits at or below a single month's receptionist salary, and answers around the clock without leave days. That said, an AI receptionist isn't a like-for-like swap for a skilled front-desk person handling awkward complaints or judgement calls — most businesses that get this right run a blended setup, with AI handling volume and routine bookings, and a clear handoff to a human when it matters. What's the price per minute or per call, in Rand?

Pricing models vary, so here's what real vendors publish, converted at roughly R16.50 to the US dollar:

  • Bland AI (developer-facing voice AI platform): $0.14 a minute on the entry tier (about R2.31), dropping to $0.11 a minute (about R1.82) on higher tiers — plus a $299–$499 a month platform fee (roughly R4,900–R8,200) once you're past the free entry level.
  • Rosie (consumer AI receptionist): $49 a month for 250 minutes (about R809) up to $299 a month for 2,000 minutes (about R4,934) — an effective R2.45–R3.25 a minute depending on plan.
  • Ruby (human virtual receptionist, for contrast): $250 a month for 50 minutes (about R4,125) up to $1,725 a month for 500 minutes (about R28,463) — an effective R57–R83 a minute, because you're paying for a live person's time, not compute.

Chat-based tools often use a different unit entirely: price per resolved conversation rather than per minute. If you're weighing up a voice agent alongside WhatsApp or web chat, expect the chat side to be billed by conversation, not by the minute. Whichever unit a vendor quotes, ask what counts as one billable unit — a missed call, a hang-up after two seconds, or a caller who rings back three times can all be billed differently depending on the platform.

Setup fees vs monthly fees: what's typical?

Three models show up repeatedly in the pricing pages we checked:

  • Pure subscription, no setup fee. Rosie's plans, for example, have no listed onboarding charge and offer a short free trial before you commit.
  • Subscription plus usage. Bland AI's higher tiers add a monthly platform fee on top of per-minute usage — you're paying for the software licence and the compute separately.
  • Build fee plus flat monthly. This is standard for custom work: a one-off charge for connecting the AI to your calendar, CRM or job-management system, then a flat monthly fee once it's live. Ruby, for its part, states plainly there's no setup, onboarding or activation fee on its standard plans — worth checking, since some vendors bury this cost.

Whichever model you're quoted, a fixed monthly fee is easier to budget against than usage that can spike with a busy month, and it's worth asking whether the quote is locked in Rand or will drift with the dollar or pound.

Quick answers

Can an AI receptionist answer calls in Afrikaans or isiZulu?

It depends on the vendor and the voice model behind it. English support is close to universal; support for Afrikaans, isiZulu, Xhosa or other South African languages varies a lot between platforms, so it's worth testing the actual accent and language handling before signing anything, not just taking a features list at face value.

Does an AI receptionist ever hand a call over to a real person?

On any properly built system, yes. A sensible setup answers routine calls itself and escalates the rest — an upset caller, an unusual request, a high-value lead — to a human, either live or via a fast callback. If a platform can't describe its handoff path clearly, that's a gap worth asking about directly.

How long does it take to set up an AI receptionist?

A simple FAQ-style setup with no integrations can go live within days. One that needs to read your calendar, write to your CRM or check stock or job availability takes longer, because it depends on API access to those systems and proper testing before it goes near real callers. Scope this honestly upfront rather than promising a launch date before the integrations are even confirmed.

What happens if call volume spikes one month?

On a usage-based plan, you simply pay for the extra minutes or calls — check the overage rate before you sign, since it's sometimes steeper than the base rate. On a flat monthly fee, ask upfront whether a reasonable volume increase is already covered or whether it triggers a renegotiation, so there's no surprise invoice after a busy month.
